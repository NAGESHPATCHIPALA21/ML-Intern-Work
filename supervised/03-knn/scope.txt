machine learning. It makes no assumptions about the underlying data distribution,
builds no explicit model during training, and yet can produce competitive results
across a wide range of tasks. Understanding KNN deeply — including its failure
modes and computational costs — builds foundational intuition for distance-based
reasoning, which appears throughout machine learning in clustering, anomaly
detection, recommendation systems, and metric learning.

4. WHAT IS KNN?
================================================================================

K-Nearest Neighbors is a non-parametric, instance-based supervised learning
algorithm. It makes predictions for a new data point by finding the K training
examples that are closest to it (its "neighbors") and aggregating their labels.

  FOR CLASSIFICATION:
  The predicted class is the majority vote among the K nearest neighbors.
  In case of a tie, the class appearing among the closest neighbors wins
  (distance-weighted voting), or the tie is broken arbitrarily.

  FOR REGRESSION:
  The predicted value is the average (or distance-weighted average) of the
  target values among the K nearest neighbors.

KEY TERMINOLOGY:

  - K: The number of neighbors to consider (the primary hyperparameter)
  - Distance Metric: The function used to measure "closeness" between points
  - Lazy Learner: KNN stores the entire training dataset and does no computation
                  during training — all computation is deferred to prediction time
  - Non-Parametric: KNN makes no assumptions about the functional form of the
                    decision boundary — it can approximate any shape
  - Instance-Based: Predictions are made by referencing actual training instances,
                    not a learned parameter vector

WHAT KNN IS NOT:
  - It is NOT a model in the traditional sense — there are no weights, no
    coefficients, no learned parameters
  - It does NOT "train" — the fit() call in Scikit-learn simply stores the data
  - It is NOT appropriate for very large datasets or real-time systems without
    additional data structures (KD-trees, Ball trees)

5. INTUITION BEHIND KNN
================================================================================

CORE INTUITION:
"Tell me who your neighbors are, and I'll tell you who you are."

This is the entire idea behind KNN. It assumes that similar data points tend to
belong to the same class or have similar target values. Closeness in feature
space implies similarity in output.

ANALOGY 1 — REAL ESTATE:
You want to estimate the price of a house you're considering buying. You don't
build a model of the entire housing market. Instead, you look at the 5 most
similar houses (same size, same neighborhood, similar age) that sold recently
and average their sale prices. That is KNN regression with K=5.

ANALOGY 2 — MEDICAL DIAGNOSIS:
A patient comes in with specific lab values. A doctor who has seen thousands of
patients doesn't build an equation. She says: "The last 7 patients with these
exact readings all had Type 2 diabetes — so this patient probably does too."
That is KNN classification with K=7.

ANALOGY 3 — RECOMMENDATION SYSTEMS:
Spotify finds users with listening histories most similar to yours (your nearest
neighbors in listening behavior space) and recommends songs those users liked
that you haven't heard yet. Large-scale KNN in action.

THE DECISION BOUNDARY:
Unlike Logistic Regression which produces a linear decision boundary, KNN
produces a boundary of arbitrary shape — it curves and adapts around the
distribution of training points. With K=1, the boundary is maximally flexible
(and noisy). With K=N (all training points), every prediction is the majority
class (maximally biased). The right K lies in between.

VISUAL INTUITION:
Imagine training data plotted on a 2D plane. For a new test point:
  - Draw a circle around it that expands until it touches K training points
  - The K points inside the circle are the neighbors
  - Their majority class label becomes the prediction
  - As K increases, the circle grows larger — more neighbors are consulted —
    and the decision becomes smoother (less sensitive to individual noisy points)

6. DISTANCE METRICS
================================================================================

Distance metrics define what "nearest" means. Choosing the wrong metric for
your data type can silently degrade performance. This is a frequent interview
and production pitfall.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6a. EUCLIDEAN DISTANCE (L2 Distance)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The straight-line distance between two points in n-dimensional space.
This is the most commonly used distance metric for continuous features.

FORMULA:
  d(p, q) = sqrt( sum_{i=1}^{n} (p_i - q_i)^2 )

SYMBOLS:
  p, q     = two data points (vectors) in n-dimensional feature space
  p_i, q_i = the i-th feature value of points p and q respectively
  n        = number of features (dimensions)
  sqrt(·)  = square root function

PROPERTIES:
  - Sensitive to the scale of features (always normalize before using)
  - Sensitive to outliers (squaring amplifies large differences)
  - Best for: continuous, normally distributed features of similar scale
  - Corresponds to the L2 norm of the difference vector: ||p - q||_2

EXAMPLE:
  Point A = [2, 3], Point B = [5, 7]
  d(A, B) = sqrt((5-2)^2 + (7-3)^2) = sqrt(9 + 16) = sqrt(25) = 5

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6b. MANHATTAN DISTANCE (L1 Distance / City Block Distance / Taxicab Distance)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The sum of absolute differences along each dimension. Named after the grid-like
street layout of Manhattan — you can only travel along axes, not diagonally.

FORMULA:
  d(p, q) = sum_{i=1}^{n} |p_i - q_i|

SYMBOLS:
  p, q     = two data points in n-dimensional feature space
  p_i, q_i = the i-th feature value of points p and q
  |·|      = absolute value
  n        = number of features

PROPERTIES:
  - More robust to outliers than Euclidean (no squaring of differences)
  - Preferred for high-dimensional spaces (less affected by the curse of
    dimensionality than Euclidean distance)
  - Best for: sparse data, high-dimensional data, grid-like feature spaces
  - Corresponds to the L1 norm: ||p - q||_1

EXAMPLE:
  Point A = [2, 3], Point B = [5, 7]
  d(A, B) = |5-2| + |7-3| = 3 + 4 = 7

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
6c. MINKOWSKI DISTANCE (Generalized Distance)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

A generalization that unifies Euclidean and Manhattan distance under a single
parameterized formula. The parameter p controls which specific distance is used.

FORMULA:
  d(x, y) = ( sum_{i=1}^{n} |x_i - y_i|^p )^(1/p)

SYMBOLS:
  x, y     = two data points in n-dimensional feature space
  x_i, y_i = the i-th feature value of points x and y
  p        = the order parameter (positive integer or real number)
  n        = number of features
  |·|      = absolute value

SPECIAL CASES:
  p = 1  -->  Manhattan Distance (L1)
  p = 2  -->  Euclidean Distance (L2)
  p = ∞  -->  Chebyshev Distance (maximum absolute difference across dimensions)

CHOOSING p:
  - p = 2 (Euclidean) is the default and works well for most problems
  - p = 1 (Manhattan) is preferred for high-dimensional or sparse data
  - p is itself a hyperparameter that can be tuned via cross-validation
  - In Scikit-learn: KNeighborsClassifier(metric='minkowski', p=2)

OTHER NOTABLE DISTANCE METRICS (beyond scope but worth knowing for interviews):
  - Cosine Similarity: Measures angle between vectors — used in NLP/text
  - Hamming Distance: For categorical or binary features
  - Mahalanobis Distance: Accounts for feature correlations and scales
  - Jaccard Distance: For set-based or binary data

7. CHOOSING THE VALUE OF K
================================================================================

K is the single most important hyperparameter in KNN. The wrong K turns a
competitive algorithm into a useless one.

THE BIAS-VARIANCE TRADEOFF IN K:

  Small K (e.g., K=1):
    - Low bias: the model memorizes training data perfectly
    - High variance: extremely sensitive to noise and individual outliers
    - Decision boundary is jagged, complex, overfitted
    - Training accuracy ≈ 100%; test accuracy may be poor

  Large K (e.g., K=N):
    - High bias: the model always predicts the majority class
    - Low variance: predictions are stable but systematically wrong
    - Decision boundary is overly smooth, underfitted
    - Every prediction is the same regardless of the input

  Optimal K:
    - Somewhere in between, found through systematic cross-validation
    - Typically odd values are preferred for binary classification to avoid ties

THE ELBOW METHOD:
  Plot validation error (y-axis) against K values (x-axis).
  The optimal K is at the "elbow" — where the error stops decreasing sharply.
  This is analogous to the elbow method in K-Means clustering.

PRACTICAL STARTING POINTS:
  - Rule of thumb: K = sqrt(N), where N is the number of training samples
    For N = 100 samples, start with K ≈ 10 and tune from there
  - Always use odd K for binary classification
  - Cross-validate over a range: K = 1, 3, 5, 7, ..., sqrt(N)

K AND CLASS IMBALANCE:
  With imbalanced classes, even a moderate K can cause the majority class to
  dominate predictions simply by outnumbering the minority class among neighbors.
  Solution: Use distance-weighted voting or resample the dataset.

DISTANCE-WEIGHTED KNN:
  Instead of equal votes, closer neighbors get higher weight.
  Weight of neighbor i: w_i = 1 / d_i   (where d_i is the distance to neighbor i)
  This naturally reduces the influence of distant neighbors.
  In Scikit-learn: KNeighborsClassifier(weights='distance')

8. STEP-BY-STEP WORKING OF KNN
================================================================================

CLASSIFICATION EXAMPLE — IS THIS EMAIL SPAM?

Setup: Training set of 1000 emails, each represented by 2 features:
       [number of exclamation marks, number of all-caps words]
       Labels: 0 = Not Spam, 1 = Spam
       K = 5

STEP 1 — STORE TRAINING DATA
  KNN performs no computation during training.
  Store all 1000 [feature vector, label] pairs in memory.
  This is what "lazy learning" means.

STEP 2 — RECEIVE A NEW DATA POINT
  New email: x_new = [12, 8]  (12 exclamation marks, 8 all-caps words)

STEP 3 — COMPUTE DISTANCES TO ALL TRAINING POINTS
  For every training point x_i, compute d(x_new, x_i) using chosen metric.
  Using Euclidean distance:
    d(x_new, x_1) = sqrt((12-10)^2 + (8-5)^2) = sqrt(4+9) = sqrt(13) ≈ 3.61
    d(x_new, x_2) = sqrt((12-3)^2 + (8-2)^2)  = sqrt(81+36) = sqrt(117) ≈ 10.82
    ... (repeat for all 1000 training points)

STEP 4 — SORT BY DISTANCE AND SELECT K NEAREST
  Sort all 1000 distances in ascending order.
  Select the top 5 (K=5) training points with smallest distances.

  Example result:
    Rank 1: distance = 1.41, label = 1 (Spam)
    Rank 2: distance = 2.24, label = 1 (Spam)
    Rank 3: distance = 3.61, label = 0 (Not Spam)
    Rank 4: distance = 4.12, label = 1 (Spam)
    Rank 5: distance = 5.00, label = 0 (Not Spam)

STEP 5 — MAJORITY VOTE
  Count votes: Spam = 3, Not Spam = 2
  Prediction: Spam (class 1)

STEP 6 — RETURN PREDICTION (AND OPTIONALLY PROBABILITY)
  Predicted label: 1 (Spam)
  Predicted probability: 3/5 = 0.60 (if soft prediction is requested)

FOR REGRESSION (same steps, different aggregation at Step 5):
  Instead of majority vote, compute mean of neighbor target values:
  If neighbor target values are [240k, 265k, 258k, 271k, 255k] (house prices),
  Prediction = (240 + 265 + 258 + 271 + 255) / 5 = 257.8k

10. ADVANTAGES AND LIMITATIONS
================================================================================

ADVANTAGES:

  1. ZERO TRAINING TIME
     KNN has no training phase. The fit() call simply stores data.
     Ideal when training data changes frequently (online/streaming settings)
     and you cannot afford to retrain a model from scratch.

  2. NO ASSUMPTIONS ABOUT DATA DISTRIBUTION
     KNN is non-parametric — it makes no assumptions about linearity,
     normality, or any functional form. It adapts to any decision boundary
     shape given sufficient data.

  3. NATURALLY HANDLES MULTI-CLASS PROBLEMS
     No modification needed for 3, 5, or 100 classes — majority voting
     works directly for any number of class labels.

  4. SIMPLE TO UNDERSTAND AND IMPLEMENT
     The algorithm can be explained to a non-technical stakeholder in under
     two minutes. Every prediction is fully traceable and auditable.

  5. INSTANTLY ADAPTS TO NEW TRAINING DATA
     Adding a new training example requires no retraining — the new point
     is simply included in future neighbor searches.

LIMITATIONS:

  1. SLOW PREDICTION ON LARGE DATASETS
     Each prediction requires computing distance to all N training points.
     Time complexity: O(N * d) per prediction, where d is dimensionality.
     With N = 1,000,000 and d = 100, each prediction requires 100 million
     operations. This is prohibitive without approximate methods (KD-trees,
     LSH — Locality Sensitive Hashing).

  2. HIGH MEMORY REQUIREMENT
     The entire training dataset must be stored in memory at prediction time.
     A dataset with 10 million rows and 500 features cannot fit in RAM on a
     standard machine.

  3. SENSITIVE TO IRRELEVANT FEATURES
     All features contribute equally to distance by default. Irrelevant features
     add noise to distance calculations, pushing relevant neighbors farther away.
     Solution: Feature selection or feature weighting before applying KNN.

  4. SENSITIVE TO FEATURE SCALE
     A feature with range [0, 10000] will dominate distance calculations over
     a feature with range [0, 1] even if the latter is more informative.
     Solution: Always standardize or normalize features before KNN.

  5. POOR PERFORMANCE IN HIGH DIMENSIONS (CURSE OF DIMENSIONALITY)
     As dimensionality increases, all points become equidistant from each other.
     "Nearest" neighbors are no longer actually near. See Section 13.

  6. IMBALANCED CLASSES CAUSE BIASED PREDICTIONS
     With K=10 in a dataset that is 90% class A and 10% class B, most
     neighborhoods will be dominated by class A regardless of the true class.

11. KNN vs LOGISTIC REGRESSION
================================================================================

Dimension                | KNN                          | Logistic Regression
-------------------------|------------------------------|----------------------------
Algorithm Type           | Instance-based, lazy         | Model-based, eager
Training Time            | O(1) — stores data only      | O(n * d * iterations)
Prediction Time          | O(N * d) — slow at scale     | O(d) — very fast
Memory (inference)       | Stores all training data     | Stores only d+1 parameters
Decision Boundary Shape  | Non-linear, arbitrary        | Linear only
Feature Scaling Needed   | YES — critical               | Helps but not critical
Handles Non-linearity    | Yes, naturally               | No (needs feature engineering)
Probability Calibration  | Poor (vote counts only)      | Excellent (sigmoid output)
Interpretability         | Medium (traceable neighbors) | High (coefficients)
Missing Data Handling    | Requires imputation          | Can be regularized around it
High Dimensional Data    | Degrades (CoD)               | Works well with regularization
Small Dataset            | Works well                   | May overfit without regularization
Class Imbalance          | Severely affected            | Affected but manageable
Best Use Case            | Local pattern recognition    | Linear separability, calibrated
                         | small-to-medium datasets     | probabilities, large scale

KEY INSIGHT FOR INTERVIEWS:
  Logistic Regression produces a global linear decision boundary — it finds the
  single best line (or hyperplane) that separates all classes. KNN produces a
  local, non-linear boundary that adapts to the density of each region.
  If the true decision boundary is linear: prefer Logistic Regression.
  If the boundary is complex and data is not high-dimensional: consider KNN.

12. KNN vs NAIVE BAYES
================================================================================

Dimension                | KNN                          | Naive Bayes
-------------------------|------------------------------|----------------------------
Algorithm Type           | Instance-based               | Probabilistic, generative
Training Time            | O(1)                         | O(N * d) — fast
Prediction Time          | O(N * d) — slow              | O(d * C) — very fast
Memory                   | Stores all training data     | Stores statistics only
Independence Assumption  | None                         | Conditional feature independence
Feature Scaling Needed   | YES — critical               | No
Handles Text/Sparse Data | Poorly                       | Excellently (Multinomial NB)
Probability Output       | Vote-based (poorly calibrated)| Native probability output
Continuous Features      | Naturally handled            | Gaussian NB assumption required
Categorical Features     | Requires encoding            | Naturally handled (Multinomial NB)
High Dimensional Data    | Degrades significantly       | Handles well
Training Data Required   | Needs more data              | Works with very small datasets
Interpretability         | Traceable neighbors          | Probability tables
Best Use Case            | Continuous features,         | Text data, small datasets,
                         | local pattern recognition    | high-dimensional sparse data

KEY INSIGHT FOR INTERVIEWS:
  Naive Bayes generalizes from training statistics — it builds a global model.
  KNN memorizes training data and reasons locally. Naive Bayes is computationally
  cheap at prediction time; KNN is expensive. For text classification, Naive Bayes
  almost always wins. For image or sensor data with continuous features, KNN is
  often competitive when dimensionality is manageable.

13. CURSE OF DIMENSIONALITY
================================================================================

This is the single most important concept for understanding when KNN fails.
Every FAANG-level ML interview involving KNN will touch on this topic.

THE CORE PROBLEM:
  In high-dimensional spaces, the concept of "nearest neighbor" breaks down.
  As the number of dimensions grows, all data points become approximately
  equidistant from each other. When all points are equally far away,
  "nearest" has no meaningful interpretation.

GEOMETRIC INTUITION:
  Consider a unit hypercube (a cube where each side has length 1) in d dimensions.
  To capture a fraction f of the training data using a hypercube centered at a
  query point, the side length of that hypercube must be:

  l = f^(1/d)

  SYMBOLS:
    l = required side length of the neighborhood
    f = fraction of total training data to capture (e.g., 0.01 for 1%)
    d = number of dimensions

  With d = 2 and f = 0.01 (capture 1% of data):
    l = 0.01^(1/2) = 0.10  --> neighborhood spans 10% of each dimension

  With d = 10 and f = 0.01:
    l = 0.01^(1/10) = 0.63 --> neighborhood spans 63% of each dimension

  With d = 100 and f = 0.01:
    l = 0.01^(1/100) = 0.955 --> neighborhood spans 95.5% of each dimension

  In 100 dimensions, to find just 1% of the training data as "neighbors,"
  you need to search 95.5% of the entire range of every feature.
  The neighborhood is no longer "local" — it encompasses nearly all the data.
  "Nearest" neighbors are almost as far away as the farthest points.

THE DISTANCE CONCENTRATION PHENOMENON:
  In high dimensions, the ratio of max-to-min distance between any set of
  random points approaches 1. All pairwise distances converge to the same value.
  This is called distance concentration, and it makes distance-based methods
  fundamentally unreliable in high-dimensional spaces.

PRACTICAL CONSEQUENCES FOR KNN:
  - Prediction accuracy degrades rapidly as d exceeds ~20-30 for typical datasets
  - The algorithm requires exponentially more data to maintain density as d grows
  - The number of samples needed to maintain the same neighbor density: N ~ r^d
    where r is the desired neighborhood radius

SOLUTIONS:
  1. DIMENSIONALITY REDUCTION: Apply PCA, t-SNE, or UMAP before KNN to project
     data into a lower-dimensional space that preserves meaningful structure.

  2. FEATURE SELECTION: Remove irrelevant features — they add distance noise
     without adding class-discriminative information.

  3. FEATURE WEIGHTING: Weight each feature by its relevance to the task
     (learned via a wrapper method or domain knowledge).

  4. USE A DIFFERENT ALGORITHM: For very high-dimensional data, switch to
     Naive Bayes, SVM (with appropriate kernel), or tree-based methods.

  5. MANHATTAN DISTANCE: Less sensitive to dimensionality than Euclidean.
     Use p=1 in Minkowski distance for high-dimensional settings.

14. FEATURE SCALING IMPORTANCE
================================================================================

Feature scaling is not optional for KNN — it is mandatory. Skipping it is the
most common production mistake and will silently destroy model performance.

WHY SCALING IS MANDATORY:
  KNN uses distance as its sole decision criterion. If features are on
  different scales, the feature with the largest numerical range will dominate
  the distance calculation — regardless of its actual predictive importance.

EXAMPLE — DIABETES PREDICTION:
  Feature 1: Glucose level, range [0, 200] mg/dL
  Feature 2: Blood pressure, range [0, 120] mmHg
  Feature 3: Number of pregnancies, range [0, 17]
  Feature 4: BMI, range [0, 67] kg/m^2

  Without scaling, glucose dominates all distance calculations because it has
  the largest numerical range. Two patients with identical health profiles
  but slightly different glucose levels appear farther apart than two patients
  with wildly different blood pressures.

TWO STANDARD APPROACHES:

  MIN-MAX NORMALIZATION (scales features to [0, 1]):

    x_scaled = (x - x_min) / (x_max - x_min)

    SYMBOLS:
      x        = original feature value
      x_min    = minimum value of the feature in the training set
      x_max    = maximum value of the feature in the training set
      x_scaled = normalized feature value (between 0 and 1)

    WHEN TO USE: When you know the feature has hard bounds (pixel intensity,
                 probability scores). Sensitive to outliers.

  STANDARDIZATION / Z-SCORE NORMALIZATION (scales to zero mean, unit variance):

    x_scaled = (x - mu) / sigma

    SYMBOLS:
      x        = original feature value
      mu       = mean of the feature in the training set
      sigma    = standard deviation of the feature in the training set
      x_scaled = standardized value (typically in range [-3, 3])

    WHEN TO USE: When features may have outliers or when you don't know the
                 bounds. Generally preferred for KNN.

CRITICAL RULE — FIT ON TRAIN, TRANSFORM TRAIN AND TEST:
  Always compute scaling parameters (min, max, mean, std) using ONLY the
  training set. Apply those same parameters to scale the test set.
  If you fit the scaler on the full dataset (including test data), you create
  data leakage — your model has seen information from the test set.

  Correct approach in Scikit-learn:
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    X_train_scaled = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)   # fit AND transform train
    X_test_scaled  = scaler.transform(X_test)         # ONLY transform test

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
15a. NumPy IMPLEMENTATION (FROM SCRATCH)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

GOAL: Build KNN without using any ML library. Only NumPy and basic Python.
      This demonstrates a deep understanding of the algorithm internals —
      exactly what FAANG interviews test in coding rounds.

COMPONENTS TO IMPLEMENT:

  1. euclidean_distance(point_a, point_b)
     - Input: two 1D NumPy arrays of equal length
     - Compute: sqrt(sum((a - b)^2))
     - Return: scalar distance value

  2. KNNClassifier class with:

     __init__(self, k=3)
       - Store K as instance variable

     fit(self, X_train, y_train)
       - Store training data as instance variables
       - No computation performed here

     predict(self, X_test)
       - For each test point x in X_test:
           a. Compute Euclidean distance from x to every training point
           b. Sort distances, get indices of K smallest
           c. Extract labels of K nearest neighbors
           d. Return the most common label (majority vote)
       - Return array of predicted labels

  3. accuracy_score(y_true, y_pred)
     - Implement from scratch: sum(y_true == y_pred) / len(y_true)

PSEUDOCODE:

  class KNNClassifier:
      def __init__(self, k):
          self.k = k

      def fit(self, X_train, y_train):
          self.X_train = X_train
          self.y_train = y_train

      def predict(self, X_test):
          predictions = []
          for x_test_point in X_test:
              distances = [euclidean(x_test_point, x_train_point)
                           for x_train_point in self.X_train]
              k_nearest_indices = argsort(distances)[:self.k]
              k_nearest_labels  = self.y_train[k_nearest_indices]
              predicted_label   = majority_vote(k_nearest_labels)
              predictions.append(predicted_label)
          return array(predictions)

KEY NUMPY FUNCTIONS TO USE:
  - np.sqrt(), np.sum()          — for distance calculation
  - np.argsort()                 — sort indices by distance (ascending)
  - np.bincount(), np.argmax()   — for majority vote among neighbor labels
  - Broadcasting: (X_train - x_test_point) ** 2  — vectorize distance over all
                                                    training points at once

16. EVALUATION METRICS
================================================================================

For the diabetes dataset (binary classification with class imbalance):

CONFUSION MATRIX:
  A 2x2 table showing:
    True Positive (TP):  Predicted diabetic, actually diabetic
    True Negative (TN):  Predicted healthy, actually healthy
    False Positive (FP): Predicted diabetic, actually healthy (Type I error)
    False Negative (FN): Predicted healthy, actually diabetic (Type II error)

ACCURACY:
  Formula: (TP + TN) / (TP + TN + FP + FN)
  Use when: Classes are balanced.
  Warning: On the diabetes dataset (~65% negative class), a model that always
           predicts "healthy" achieves 65% accuracy while being completely useless.

PRECISION:
  Formula: TP / (TP + FP)
  Interpretation: Of all patients we flagged as diabetic, how many actually are?
  Use when: False positives are costly (e.g., unnecessary invasive procedures)

RECALL (SENSITIVITY):
  Formula: TP / (TP + FN)
  Interpretation: Of all patients who actually have diabetes, how many did we catch?
  Use when: False negatives are costly (e.g., missing a diabetes diagnosis)
  For medical applications: RECALL IS THE PRIMARY METRIC

F1 SCORE:
  Formula: 2 * (Precision * Recall) / (Precision + Recall)
  Interpretation: Harmonic mean of precision and recall.
  Use when: You need a single metric that balances both concerns.
  Use for imbalanced datasets.

ROC-AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve):
  The ROC curve plots True Positive Rate (Recall) vs False Positive Rate at
  various decision thresholds.
  AUC = 1.0: Perfect classifier
  AUC = 0.5: Random classifier (no better than flipping a coin)
  AUC = 0.0: Perfect inverse (predicts the wrong class every time)
  Use when: You want a threshold-independent measure of ranking quality.

CROSS-VALIDATION:
  Use k-fold cross-validation (k=5 or k=10) to get a more reliable estimate
  of generalization performance than a single train/test split.
  Report mean ± standard deviation across folds.

SYMBOLS USED ACROSS ALL METRICS:
  TP = True Positives  (correct positive predictions)
  TN = True Negatives  (correct negative predictions)
  FP = False Positives (incorrect positive predictions — "false alarms")
  FN = False Negatives (incorrect negative predictions — "missed cases")

17. HYPERPARAMETERS AND THEIR SIGNIFICANCE
================================================================================

1. n_neighbors (K)
   - Most important hyperparameter
   - Controls bias-variance tradeoff
   - Small K = low bias, high variance (overfitting)
   - Large K = high bias, low variance (underfitting)
   - Tune via cross-validation; use odd values for binary classification
   - Rule of thumb starting point: K = sqrt(N_train)

2. metric / p (Distance Metric)
   - Controls how "distance" is measured
   - 'minkowski' with p=2: Euclidean (default, best for most problems)
   - 'minkowski' with p=1: Manhattan (better for high-dimensional or sparse data)
   - 'cosine': For text or normalized vector data
   - 'hamming': For binary or categorical features
   - Tune via cross-validation if unsure

3. weights
   - 'uniform': All K neighbors vote equally
   - 'distance': Closer neighbors vote with higher weight (1/distance)
   - Distance weighting reduces sensitivity to the exact choice of K
   - Generally improves performance when decision boundary is complex

4. algorithm
   - Controls the data structure used to search for neighbors
   - 'brute': Compute distance to every training point — O(N*d) per query
              Use for small datasets (N < 10,000)
   - 'kd_tree': Recursive binary space partitioning — O(d*log(N)) per query
                Use for low-dimensional data (d < 20)
   - 'ball_tree': Spherical partitioning — O(d*log(N)) per query
                  More effective than KD-tree for higher dimensions (d < 50)
   - 'auto': Scikit-learn picks the best option automatically

5. leaf_size
   - Applies to 'kd_tree' and 'ball_tree'
   - Controls when the tree switches to brute-force search (at leaf nodes)
   - Affects memory usage and query speed
   - Default of 30 is usually fine; tune only if speed is critical

6. n_jobs
   - Number of parallel CPU jobs for distance computation
   - n_jobs = -1 uses all available cores
   - Significant speedup on multicore machines for large datasets

18. TOP 10 INTERVIEW QUESTIONS
================================================================================

Q1. Explain the KNN algorithm in your own words. What happens during training
    and what happens during prediction?

A:  KNN is a lazy learning algorithm — it performs no computation during
    training beyond storing the training data. All computation is deferred to
    prediction time. When a new data point arrives, KNN computes its distance
    to every training point, selects the K closest points (neighbors), and
    returns the majority class (for classification) or the mean target value
    (for regression). The key insight is that there is no explicit model — the
    training data itself is the model.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q2. Why must you scale features before applying KNN?

A:  KNN uses distance as its sole decision criterion. If features are on
    different scales, the feature with the largest numerical range dominates
    all distance calculations regardless of its actual predictive importance.
    For example, if one feature ranges from 0 to 10,000 and another ranges
    from 0 to 1, the first feature will contribute 10,000x more to the
    Euclidean distance than the second, even if the second feature is far more
    informative. Standardization or normalization brings all features to
    comparable scales and ensures each feature contributes proportionally.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q3. How does the choice of K affect model performance? What is the bias-variance
    tradeoff in the context of KNN?

A:  K = 1 gives a model with maximum flexibility — it assigns the exact label
    of the nearest training point, memorizing the training data perfectly but
    being highly sensitive to noise (high variance, low bias). Increasing K
    smooths the decision boundary — the model considers more neighbors and
    becomes less sensitive to individual noisy points (lower variance) but may
    miss fine-grained patterns (higher bias). K = N (all training points)
    produces a model that always predicts the majority class (maximum bias).
    The optimal K balances these two forces and is found via cross-validation.
    A validation curve plotting error vs. K typically shows an "elbow" at the
    optimal value.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q4. What is the curse of dimensionality and how does it affect KNN specifically?

A:  In high-dimensional spaces, all data points become approximately equidistant
    from each other — a phenomenon known as distance concentration. Formally, to
    find just 1% of training data as "neighbors" in 100 dimensions, you must
    search over 95.5% of the range of every feature. The neighborhood is no longer
    local. This means "nearest neighbors" are not actually similar to the query
    point, making predictions unreliable. KNN is uniquely vulnerable to this
    because its entire decision-making mechanism depends on meaningful distance.
    Solutions include PCA for dimensionality reduction, feature selection, or
    switching to algorithms that don't rely on distance (tree-based methods).

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q5. Compare KNN to Logistic Regression. When would you use each?

A:  Logistic Regression is a parametric, discriminative model that learns a global
    linear decision boundary. It is fast to predict (O(d) per prediction), requires
    minimal memory (stores d+1 parameters), produces well-calibrated probabilities,
    and works well in high-dimensional spaces with regularization. KNN is
    non-parametric and lazy — it makes no assumptions about the decision boundary
    shape and can model arbitrarily complex patterns. However, it is slow to predict
    (O(N*d) per prediction), stores the entire dataset, and degrades in high
    dimensions. Use Logistic Regression when: the decision boundary is approximately
    linear, you need calibrated probabilities, or data is high-dimensional. Use KNN
    when: the decision boundary is highly non-linear, the dataset is small to medium,
    and features are continuous and meaningful.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q6. What is the zero-training-time property of KNN and what are its practical
    implications?

A:  KNN is a lazy learner — it performs no computation during fit(). This means
    training is O(1) in time. The practical implication is that KNN adapts
    instantly to new data: adding a new training example requires no retraining.
    This makes KNN attractive for online learning scenarios where training data
    changes frequently. However, the cost is paid entirely at prediction time,
    making KNN impractical for real-time inference at scale. The tradeoff is
    training time vs. prediction time — inverse of how eager learners (like
    Logistic Regression) behave.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q7. How does KNN handle multi-class classification?

A:  KNN naturally extends to any number of classes without modification. The
    prediction is determined by majority vote among the K nearest neighbors.
    Whichever class appears most frequently among the neighbors is predicted.
    For regression with multiple targets, the mean of each target dimension
    is computed independently. No one-vs-rest or one-vs-one decomposition is
    needed, unlike some algorithms (SVM) that require such modifications.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q8. How would you handle class imbalance in KNN?

A:  Class imbalance causes KNN to be biased toward the majority class because
    majority-class examples dominate the neighborhoods of most query points.
    Solutions include:
    (1) Distance-weighted voting (weights='distance') — closer minority-class
        neighbors gain more influence even when outnumbered
    (2) Resampling — oversample the minority class (SMOTE) or undersample
        the majority class before training
    (3) Adjusting the decision threshold — predict the minority class when its
        vote count exceeds a fraction lower than 50%
    (4) Using evaluation metrics that account for imbalance (F1, AUC-ROC)
        rather than accuracy

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q9. What is the time and space complexity of KNN? How would you scale it for
    very large datasets?

A:  Naive KNN complexity:
    - Training: O(1) time, O(N*d) space (stores training data)
    - Prediction: O(N*d) time per query (computes distance to all N points)
    Total prediction for M test points: O(M*N*d)

    For large datasets, use approximate nearest neighbor methods:
    - KD-Tree: O(d*log(N)) per query but degrades above ~20 dimensions
    - Ball Tree: Similar to KD-tree but handles higher dimensions better
    - Locality Sensitive Hashing (LSH): Sub-linear approximate search
    - FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search): GPU-accelerated ANN for billions
      of vectors — used in production recommendation systems at Meta
    Scikit-learn's algorithm='auto' selects KD-tree or Ball tree automatically
    based on N and d.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q10. Can KNN be used for regression? How does it differ from KNN classification?

A:  Yes. KNN regression follows the same neighbor-finding procedure but differs
    in how neighbors' outputs are aggregated. In classification, the majority class
    label among K neighbors is returned. In regression, the mean (or
    distance-weighted mean) of the K neighbors' target values is returned.
    KNN regression produces a step-function approximation to the true relationship
    — predictions are piecewise constant (in 1D) or piecewise constant over
    Voronoi cells (in higher dimensions). It captures local non-linear patterns
    well but is noisy with small K and overly smooth with large K. Same tradeoffs
    as classification: K controls bias-variance, scaling is mandatory, and
    performance degrades with dimensionality.

19. COMMON MISTAKES STUDENTS MAKE
================================================================================

MISTAKE 1 — NOT SCALING FEATURES
  Symptom: Model achieves mediocre accuracy that doesn't improve with tuning.
  Fix: Always apply StandardScaler or MinMaxScaler BEFORE KNN.
  Rule: If you write KNN code and StandardScaler isn't in it, something is wrong.

MISTAKE 2 — FITTING THE SCALER ON THE FULL DATASET (DATA LEAKAGE)
  Symptom: Suspiciously high test accuracy that doesn't hold in production.
  Fix: Fit the scaler only on training data. Use a Pipeline to enforce this.
  Explanation: Fitting on full data leaks test-set statistics into the scaler,
               making the test set artificially "easier" than real new data.

MISTAKE 3 — CHOOSING K = 1 WITHOUT JUSTIFICATION
  Symptom: Perfect training accuracy, poor test accuracy.
  Fix: Tune K via cross-validation. Start with K = sqrt(N_train).
  Rule: K=1 is almost always overfit. Never deploy a K=1 model without scrutiny.

MISTAKE 4 — USING EVEN K FOR BINARY CLASSIFICATION
  Symptom: Unpredictable tie-breaking behavior, inconsistent results.
  Fix: Always use odd K values (3, 5, 7, ...) for binary classification.
  Why: Odd K ensures there's always a majority class, eliminating ties.

MISTAKE 5 — IGNORING HIGH DIMENSIONALITY
  Symptom: KNN performs poorly despite tuning, worse than simpler models.
  Fix: Check number of features. If d > 30, apply PCA or feature selection first.
  Rule: KNN is not appropriate as-is for truly high-dimensional data.

MISTAKE 6 — CONFUSING K IN KNN WITH K IN K-MEANS
  Symptom: Conceptual confusion in interviews or explanations.
  Fix: KNN is a supervised learning algorithm — K is the number of neighbors.
       K-Means is an unsupervised clustering algorithm — K is the number of clusters.
       They are completely different algorithms sharing a letter.

MISTAKE 7 — EVALUATING ONLY ACCURACY ON IMBALANCED DATA
  Symptom: 65% accuracy on diabetes dataset and calling it good.
  Fix: Always report precision, recall, F1, and AUC-ROC for imbalanced data.
       A model that always predicts the majority class gets 65% accuracy for free.

MISTAKE 8 — NOT USING CROSS-VALIDATION FOR K SELECTION
  Symptom: Model performs well on one train/test split but poorly on another.
  Fix: Use cross_val_score with cv=5 or cv=10 to get robust estimates.
       Plot K vs. mean validation accuracy ± std to select the optimal K.

MISTAKE 9 — APPLYING KNN TO TEXT DATA WITHOUT APPROPRIATE SIMILARITY
  Symptom: Very poor performance on NLP tasks.
  Fix: Text similarity is better measured with cosine similarity on TF-IDF vectors,
       not Euclidean distance on raw word counts. Or use Naive Bayes for text.

MISTAKE 10 — ASSUMING KNN IS ALWAYS INTERPRETABLE
  Symptom: Reporting KNN as an interpretable model to stakeholders.
  Fix: KNN predictions are *traceable* (you can see which neighbors influenced
       the prediction) but not *interpretable* in the coefficient sense. You cannot
       say "feature X contributes Y amount to the prediction." For feature-level
       interpretability, use SHAP values or switch to Logistic Regression.

    - Non-linear decision boundaries
    - Continuous features
    - Dimensionality is manageable (d < 30)

    - Very large datasets (slow prediction)

    - Class-imbalanced data without additional handling

21. KEY TAKEAWAYS
================================================================================

  1. KNN is the purest expression of the "learning by analogy" paradigm in ML.
     It makes no assumptions, builds no model, and reasons entirely from the
     stored training data. Understanding KNN deeply prepares you to think clearly
     about similarity, distance, and local structure — concepts that appear across
     all of machine learning.

  2. Feature scaling is not a best practice for KNN — it is a hard requirement.
     An unscaled KNN implementation is a broken implementation. This is the most
     commonly tested KNN gotcha in FAANG interviews.

  3. The bias-variance tradeoff is expressed entirely through K. This makes KNN
     an excellent teaching tool for understanding the tradeoff intuitively:
     K=1 is the purest overfitter; K=N is the purest underfitter.

  4. The curse of dimensionality is KNN's fundamental limitation. Every interview
     about KNN will probe this. Know it conceptually, mathematically (the l = f^(1/d)
     formula), and practically (solutions: PCA, feature selection, switch algorithms).

  5. KNN's zero-training-time property is a real competitive advantage in scenarios
     where data changes frequently. But this comes at the cost of slow, memory-
     intensive prediction. Understanding this tradeoff is essential for system design.

  6. In production ML, raw KNN is rarely deployed at scale. Its algorithmic variants
     (KD-trees, Ball trees, FAISS, LSH) are everywhere: in recommendation systems,
     image search, and embedding-based retrieval systems. Knowing KNN is knowing
     the foundation of these systems.

  7. Implementing KNN from scratch in NumPy is one of the clearest demonstrations
     of algorithmic understanding in an ML interview. It requires no complex math —
     just clean thinking about distances, sorting, and voting.
